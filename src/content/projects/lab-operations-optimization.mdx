## Executive summary

A global laboratory network runs work across multiple processing sites, each with its own
capacity, queue, and turnaround commitments. When demand shifts, leaders face a hard question:
**where should work be routed, when does a site tip into critical backlog, and which transfers
actually help versus just move the problem?** As technical lead, I designed a decision-support
framework — a two-stage optimization paired with Monte Carlo simulation — that lets senior
stakeholders test capacity, routing, and backlog scenarios before committing to them. The model
was validated against confidential client operating data and its recommendations were presented to
senior and executive stakeholders as an operational planning tool, not a one-off analysis.

## Business context

A multi-site service network is deceptively hard to reason about. Each site looks fine in
isolation, but the network behaves as a coupled system: a spike at one site spills into transfers,
transfers consume capacity elsewhere, and turnaround commitments are missed two steps away from
the original bottleneck. Leaders were making capacity and routing calls on spreadsheets and
intuition, with no way to see how a decision would ripple under uncertain demand.

## The core decision

The framework was built to support a specific set of recurring operational decisions:

- **Routing** — where should incoming work be sent given current load and turnaround targets?
- **Capacity thresholds** — at what utilization does a site cross from busy into critical backlog?
- **Transfers** — how do inter-site transfers change backlog and turnaround, and when do they backfire?
- **Prioritization** — which operational interventions give the most service-level improvement per unit of capacity?
- **Robustness** — how do the recommended decisions change once demand uncertainty is modeled explicitly?

## My role

I owned the analytical architecture end to end: framing the problem into a solvable model,
defining the two-stage formulation and simulation logic, coordinating the team's technical work,
validating outputs against client data in a secure environment, and translating results into
executive-ready recommendations. My focus was making a technically sophisticated model
**legible and decision-relevant** to non-technical operating leaders.

## Analytical framework

The system has two layers that answer two different questions:

- **Stage one — allocation & routing (optimization).** A two-stage optimization decides how to
  allocate capacity and route work across sites subject to real operating constraints, balancing
  throughput against turnaround commitments.
- **Stage two — uncertainty (Monte Carlo simulation).** Demand and processing variability are
  sampled across many scenarios so recommendations are evaluated on their *distribution* of
  outcomes, not a single point estimate. A decision that looks optimal on average but fails badly
  in the tail is surfaced, not hidden.

The two layers together let a leader ask *"what if demand runs 15% hot at these two sites"* and see
the routing, backlog, and service-level consequences before it happens.

## Key findings

*Specific figures are withheld under NDA; the findings below are described qualitatively.*

- The recommended routing policy improved modeled on-time turnaround relative to the status-quo allocation.
- Backlog concentrated at a small number of constrained sites — and adding capacity *there* relieved the whole network far more than spreading capacity evenly.
- A clear utilization threshold emerged, beyond which service level degraded sharply rather than gradually — a useful planning line for "how hot is too hot."
- Modeling demand uncertainty explicitly changed which interventions looked best: several decisions that were optimal on average proved fragile in the tail.

## Recommendations

Analysis was translated into a prioritized set of operational moves — which sites to shore up
first, which transfer rules to change, and which scenarios warranted contingency planning —
framed explicitly around the tradeoff between **speed, capacity, and service reliability** so leaders
could choose with the tradeoffs visible.

## Validation & limitations

Model outputs were validated against confidential client operating data in a secure environment.
As with any operational model, recommendations are conditional on the demand assumptions and the
fidelity of transfer/queue rules; the Monte Carlo layer is included precisely so decisions are
stress-tested against uncertainty rather than a single forecast.

## Business impact

The deliverable was not a number — it was **repeatable decision support**: a way for leaders to
evaluate capacity, routing, and backlog scenarios and align on a plan before committing resources,
with the tradeoffs made explicit and defensible.

## What I learned

- Modeling an interconnected operational system, where the bottleneck and the symptom are rarely
  the same place.
- Balancing model complexity against usability — a model executives won't use has no impact.
- Translating optimization and simulation output into language an operating leader acts on.
- Leading technical work across a team toward a single, coherent recommendation.

- An interactive decision dashboard over the simulation outputs.
- Continuous model calibration as new operating data arrives.
- An AI-assisted layer to answer operational questions against the model in plain language.
